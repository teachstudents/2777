Chefchaouen, Morocco

Vocabulary

  1. medina /məˈdiːnə/: The old medina is a labyrinth of narrow streets filled with shops and houses.
  2. azure /ˈæʒər/: The sky was a brilliant azure, without a single cloud.
  3. Rif Mountains /rɪf ˈmaʊntɪnz/: The city is nestled beautifully in the Rif Mountains.
  4. tranquil /ˈtræŋkwɪl/: He led a tranquil life in the quiet countryside.
  5. artisan /ˌɑːrtɪˈzæn/: Local artisan shops sell handmade leather goods and woven blankets.
  6. kasbah /ˈkæzbɑː/: The historic kasbah overlooks the entire city and the valley below.
  7. palette /ˈpælət/: The artist used a vibrant palette of colors in her painting.
  8. picturesque /ˌpɪktʃəˈresk/: We stayed in a picturesque village with charming, traditional houses.
  9. custom /ˈkʌstəm/: It is a local custom to welcome guests with a cup of mint tea.
  10. refugee /ˈrefjʊdʒiː/: The blue color was supposedly introduced by Jewish refugees.
  11. spiritual /ˈspɪrɪtʃuəl/: For many, the color blue has a deep spiritual meaning.
  12. tagine /təˈʒiːn/: We enjoyed a delicious lamb tagine, a slow-cooked Moroccan stew.
  13. vibrant /ˈvaɪbrənt/: The marketplace was vibrant, filled with sounds, smells, and colors.
  14. textiles /ˈtekstaɪlz/: The region is famous for its colorful textiles and carpets.
  15. berber /ˈbɜːrbər/: The indigenous Berber culture has a strong influence on the region.
  16. mountainous /ˈmaʊntənəs/: The mountainous terrain makes farming difficult but offers stunning views.
  17. aesthetic /esˈθetɪk/: The unique aesthetic of the town attracts photographers from all over.
  18. serenity /səˈrenəti/: Visitors come seeking the peace and serenity of the blue city.
  19. municipality /mjuːˌnɪsɪˈpæləti/: The entire municipality is recognized by UNESCO for its cultural significance.
  20. hospitable /hɒˈspɪtəbəl/: The local people are known for being incredibly warm and hospitable.

Warm-up Questions

  1. In Chinese culture, colors have deep meanings, like red for luck and white for mourning. The city of Chefchaouen is famously blue. What feelings or ideas do you associate with the color blue?
  2. This city is built in a mountainous area, similar to regions in Yunnan or Sichuan. How do you think living in the mountains might shape a community's character and daily life?
  3. Chefchaouen's unique look comes from a historical tradition started by refugees. Can you think of a tradition or style in a Chinese city that comes from a specific historical event?
  4. The article mentions a blend of Berber, Arab, and Andalusian cultures. Where in China have you seen a similar blending of different ethnic or regional cultures? What was the result?
  5. Imagine your city decided to adopt a single color for all its buildings. Which color would you choose to represent your city's identity, and why?

Reading Passage

Chefchaouen is a city in Morocco. It is in the Rif Mountains, which makes the city very beautiful. The area is mountainous and the city is small and tranquil. Inside the city, there is an old kasbah, which is a type of fortress. The city is famous for its blue color. The buildings in the old part of the city, the medina, are painted in a lovely azure blue.

The city’s unique aesthetic, which attracts many photographers, has a rich history. The custom of painting the walls blue was reportedly started by a Jewish refugee community that arrived centuries ago. For them, the color had a deep spiritual meaning, and consequently, it gives the whole city a feeling of serenity. The local culture is a vibrant mix of indigenous Berber and Arab traditions. This is evident in the many artisan shops, which sell handmade textiles, and in the local food, such as the slow-cooked tagine.

This picturesque location offers a peaceful experience, especially when contrasted with Morocco's larger, more chaotic imperial cities. A clear cause-and-effect relationship is shown: the historical influx of refugees directly caused the city’s unique blue palette. However, this fame has led to a modern struggle; the very identity that makes it a popular tourist destination is now threatened by overcrowding. Despite this, the famously hospitable local people maintain their culture, and the entire municipality is recognized by UNESCO for its significance.

Nuuk, Greenland

Vocabulary

  1. arctic /ˈɑːrktɪk/: Polar bears are perfectly adapted to the harsh arctic environment.
  2. fjord /fjɔːrd/: Nuuk is situated at the mouth of a spectacular fjord system.
  3. ice sheet /ˈaɪs ʃiːt/: The immense Greenland ice sheet is melting at an alarming rate.
  4. indigenous /ɪnˈdɪdʒənəs/: The museum showcases the culture of the indigenous Inuit people.
  5. autonomous /ɔːˈtɒnəməs/: Greenland is an autonomous constituent country within the Kingdom of Denmark.
  6. contemporary /kənˈtempərəri/: The city has a vibrant contemporary art and music scene.
  7. tundra /ˈtʌndrə/: The surrounding landscape is a vast, treeless arctic tundra.
  8. aurora borealis /əˌrɔːrə ˌbɔːriˈælɪs/: In winter, you can often see the aurora borealis from Nuuk.
  9. harbor /ˈhɑːrbər/: The colorful buildings around the old colonial harbor are very photogenic.
  10. governance /ˈɡʌvərnəns/: The city is the seat of Greenland's self-governance.
  11. striking /ˈstraɪkɪŋ/: The Katuaq Cultural Centre is a piece of striking modern architecture.
  12. suaasat /suˈɑːsæt/: A traditional Greenlandic soup, suaasat is often made with seal or reindeer meat.
  13. climate change /ˈklaɪmət tʃeɪndʒ/: The effects of climate change are very visible in the arctic.
  14. tradition /trəˈdɪʃən/: The city blends modern life with ancient Inuit tradition.
  15. remote /rɪˈməʊt/: Despite being a capital, Nuuk feels very remote from the rest of the world.
  16. subarctic /ˌsʌbˈɑːrktɪk/: Nuuk has a maritime-influenced subarctic climate with cold, snowy winters.
  17. peninsula /pəˈnɪnsjələ/: The city is located at the end of a large peninsula.
  18. microbrewery /ˌmaɪkrəʊˈbruːəri/: Nuuk is home to a popular microbrewery using local ingredients.
  19. heritage /ˈherɪtɪdʒ/: The Greenland National Museum preserves the country's rich heritage.
  20. cosmopolitan /ˌkɒzməˈpɒlɪtən/: For its size, Nuuk is a surprisingly cosmopolitan city.

Warm-up Questions

  1. Nuuk is a very remote capital city. Can you think of any remote or isolated cities in China? What are the biggest challenges and unique benefits of living in such a place?
  2. The article describes Nuuk as a mix of indigenous Inuit and modern Danish culture. China has 56 officially recognized ethnic groups. Describe how a minority culture you know of has blended with modern Han culture.
  3. The people of Nuuk have a very close relationship with nature (the sea, the ice, the animals). How is this similar to or different from the relationship people have with nature in a major Chinese city like Beijing or Shanghai?
  4. The buildings in Nuuk's harbor are very colorful. Do you think the color of buildings affects the mood of a city's residents? Why or why not?
  5. If you had to design a new "national dish" for China that everyone could enjoy, what ingredients would you include to represent the country's diversity?

Reading Passage

Nuuk is the capital of Greenland, which is an autonomous country. It is a very remote city in the arctic. It sits on a peninsula next to a large fjord. The land around the city is treeless tundra. The climate is subarctic, which means it has long, cold winters. The city is also the center of Greenland's governance and home to its indigenous Inuit population.

The city's culture is a fascinating mix of ancient Inuit tradition and contemporary Danish life, which makes it a surprisingly cosmopolitan place. For instance, you can find modern art galleries and even a microbrewery near the old colonial harbor. The National Museum, which preserves the country's rich heritage, is a popular attraction. A traditional food that people eat here is suaasat, a type of meat soup. At night in the winter, you can often see the beautiful aurora borealis.

The city presents a striking contrast between its small population and its global importance. A key cause-and-effect relationship exists between global warming and the local environment; as a result of rising temperatures, the massive Greenland ice sheet is melting, which creates both local challenges and global consequences. Unlike sprawling inland capitals, Nuuk is compact and its life is defined by the sea. This community faces the immense struggle of adapting to climate change while balancing its identity, which is evident in the blend of traditional hunting culture and modern, forward-thinking architecture.

Oymyakon, Russia

Vocabulary

  1. inhabited /ɪnˈhæbɪtɪd/: Oymyakon is one of the coldest permanently inhabited places on Earth.
  2. extreme /ɪkˈstriːm/: Residents must cope with extreme winter temperatures.
  3. permafrost /ˈpɜːrməfrɒst/: Most buildings are built on stilts due to the unstable permafrost.
  4. Siberia /saɪˈbɪəriə/: The village is located deep in the heart of Siberia.
  5. subzero /ˌsʌbˈzɪərəʊ/: Subzero temperatures are normal for more than half the year.
  6. endurance /ɪnˈdjʊərəns/: Living in Oymyakon requires incredible endurance and adaptation.
  7. thermal /ˈθɜːrməl/: People wear multiple layers of thermal clothing to stay warm.
  8. Indigirka /ɪnˈdiːɡɪrkə/: The village is situated along the Indigirka River.
  9. antifreeze /ˈæntifriːz/: Cars must be kept running or they will freeze solid.
  10. mercury /ˈmɜːrkjəri/: It is so cold that mercury in thermometers can freeze.
  11. hardy /ˈhɑːrdi/: Only the hardiest of animals and people can survive here.
  12. isolated /ˈaɪsəleɪtɪd/: The village is extremely isolated, hundreds of kilometers from any major city.
  13. logistics /ləˈdʒɪstɪks/: The logistics of getting supplies to the village are very complex.
  14. Sakha /sɑːˈhɑː/: Oymyakon is in the Sakha Republic, also known as Yakutia.
  15. brutal /ˈbruːtəl/: The winter climate is nothing short of brutal.
  16. pole of cold /pəʊl əv kəʊld/: Oymyakon is known as the Northern Hemisphere's "Pole of Cold."
  17. adaptation /ˌædæpˈteɪʃən/: Their lifestyle is a remarkable story of human adaptation.
  18. stroganina /strɒɡəˈniːnə/: A local delicacy is stroganina, which consists of raw, frozen fish sliced thinly.
  19. daylight /ˈdeɪlaɪt/: In December, there are only about three hours of daylight.
  20. valley /ˈvæli/: The geography of the valley traps cold air, making it colder than surrounding areas.

Warm-up Questions

  1. The people of Oymyakon must show great endurance. Describe a time you or someone you know had to endure a difficult situation (like studying for the Gaokao or a challenging physical task). What qualities were needed?
  2. In Oymyakon, the community must be very tight-knit to survive. How important is the concept of "community" or neighborhood (邻里) in modern Chinese cities compared to the past?
  3. The diet in Oymyakon is based on what is available (meat and fish). How does the geography of your region in China influence the local food you eat?
  4. Imagine living with only three hours of daylight in winter. How do you think this would affect your daily schedule, mood, and social life?
  5. The article calls Oymyakon "isolated." With high-speed rail and planes, is any part of China still truly isolated today? Discuss.

Reading Passage

Oymyakon is a small village in the Sakha Republic of Russia, in Siberia. It is the coldest permanently inhabited place on Earth. Because of this, it is called the "Pole of Cold." The village is located in a valley next to the Indigirka River. This geography traps cold air. The weather is extreme and winters are very long and brutal. The ground is always frozen, which is called permafrost.

Because the environment is so difficult, the village is very isolated, and the logistics of getting supplies are complex. The few hundred people who live there are very hardy and show incredible endurance. Their entire lifestyle is a remarkable adaptation to the cold. For example, they must wear special thermal clothing, and cars are often left running so the engines don't freeze and require antifreeze. During the deep winter, there are only a few hours of daylight, and the subzero temperatures are so low that mercury in thermometers can freeze.

This location provides a powerful example of human survival. A direct cause-and-effect relationship is evident: the extreme cold is the cause, and the unique local culture, diet, and technology are the effects. In contrast to life in a modern, temperate city, life in Oymyakon is a constant struggle against nature, and its small population has declined over the years. The community's resilience is remarkable, as shown in their diet of local specialties like stroganina (raw, frozen fish) and their celebration of the "Pole of Cold" festival, which turns a harsh reality into a source of cultural pride and identity.

Iquitos, Peru

Vocabulary

  1. inaccessible /ˌɪnəkˈsesəbəl/: Iquitos is the largest city in the world that is inaccessible by road.
  2. Amazon Rainforest /ˈæməzɒn ˈreɪnfɒrɪst/: The city is an urban oasis in the vast Amazon Rainforest.
  3. bustling /ˈbʌslɪŋ/: The port is always bustling with boats of all sizes.
  4. river port /ˈrɪvər pɔːrt/: As a major river port, it connects the region to the world.
  5. biodiversity /ˌbaɪəʊdaɪˈvɜːrsəti/: The surrounding jungle has incredible biodiversity.
  6. gateway /ˈɡeɪtweɪ/: Iquitos serves as the gateway for jungle lodges and river cruises.
  7. exotic /ɪɡˈzɒtɪk/: The Belen Market sells exotic fruits and local remedies.
  8. stilt houses /ˈstɪlt haʊzɪz/: Many families in the Belen district live in stilt houses over the river.
  9. rubber boom /ˈrʌbər buːm/: The city's grand mansions were built during the 19th-century rubber boom.
  10. motocarro /ˌmoʊtoʊˈkɑːroʊ/: The most common form of transport is the noisy motocarro.
  11. confluence /ˈkɒnfluəns/: The city is near the confluence of several major rivers.
  12. equatorial /ˌiːkwəˈtɔːriəl/: The city has a hot and humid equatorial climate.
  13. aquatic /əˈkwætɪk/: The floating market is a center for aquatic commerce.
  14. juane /wɑːˈneɪ/: A popular local dish is juane, which consists of rice, chicken, and spices wrapped in a banana leaf.
  15. tropical /ˈtrɒpɪkəl/: The climate is hot and humid, typical of a tropical rainforest.
  16. architecture /ˌɑːrkɪˈtektʃər/: The architecture reflects a mix of colonial splendor and jungle practicality.
  17. isolated /ˈaɪsəleɪtɪd/: Its isolated location has fostered a unique and self-sufficient culture.
  18. tributaries /ˈtrɪbjətəriz/: The Amazon River is fed by thousands of smaller tributaries.
  19. shamanism /ˈʃɑːmənɪzəm/: The region is a center for Amazonian shamanism and traditional plant medicine.
  20. meandering /miˈændərɪŋ/: The river follows a slow, meandering course through the jungle.

Warm-up Questions

  1. Iquitos is defined by its relationship with the Amazon River. Think of a major river in China, like the Yangtze or Yellow River. How has that river shaped the identity and economy of a city on its banks (e.g., Wuhan, Chongqing, Shanghai)?
  2. The city has grand European mansions next to simple stilt houses. What does this contrast in architecture tell you about the city's history and social structure? Can you see similar contrasts in Chinese cities?
  3. The main transport in Iquitos is a three-wheeled motocarro. What unique or common forms of transportation define daily life in your city (e.g., electric bikes, subways, shared bikes)? What do they say about the city?
  4. The article mentions a "rubber boom." China has experienced its own economic booms, for example, in manufacturing or technology. How do such booms change a city and the lives of its people?
  5. The Belén Market sells exotic fruits and traditional medicines from the jungle. What kinds of special local products or traditional remedies can be found in the markets of your region?

Reading Passage

Iquitos is a large city in Peru. It is located deep in the Amazon Rainforest. The city is very isolated and inaccessible by road; you can only get there by plane or boat. Iquitos is a bustling river port on the Amazon River, which has a slow, meandering course. The weather is always hot and wet because it has a tropical, equatorial climate. The city is the main gateway to the jungle.

The city's history is famous for a big rubber boom over 100 years ago, which is why you can still see some grand European architecture today. However, this is mixed with local structures like the stilt houses of the Belén Market, which has an aquatic section that floats on the river. The culture is a mix of indigenous and colonial traditions, and the region is a center for Amazonian shamanism. A popular local food is juane, and the market sells many exotic fruits. The most common transport is the noisy three-wheeled motocarro.

This city presents a striking paradox: it is a major urban center that is completely disconnected from any road network, a direct result of its jungle location. This geography is the cause of its unique development, which in turn has created a culture of self-sufficiency. In contrast to cities that expand along highways, Iquitos's lifeblood flows through its river tributaries and its port at the confluence of mighty rivers. Despite its historical wealth from the rubber trade, the city today struggles with poverty. This is evident in the contrast between the few remaining grand mansions and the widespread poverty in areas like Belén, highlighting a challenging social dynamic in this gateway to the Amazon's incredible biodiversity.

New Orleans, USA

  2. jazz /dʒæz/: New Orleans is widely considered the birthplace of jazz music.
  3. Mardi Gras /ˈmɑːrdi ɡrɑː/: The annual Mardi Gras celebration is a world-famous festival of parades and parties.
  4. bayou /ˈbaɪuː/: The city is surrounded by a network of swamps and bayous.
  5. resilience /rɪˈzɪliəns/: The people showed incredible resilience after Hurricane Katrina.

Warm-up Questions

  1. New Orleans is called a "melting pot" of cultures. Which city in China do you think is the best example of a cultural melting pot, and why?

Reading Passage

New Orleans is a city in Louisiana, USA. It is on the Mississippi River Delta, near the sea. The city is surrounded by swamps and bayou landscapes. Because it is low, it is protected by a system of walls called a levee. The climate is subtropical. The city is also a parish, which is like a county. It is a very multicultural place with a long history.

The city's culture is a unique Creole style, which is a convergence of European, African, and Caribbean traditions, making it a true cultural melting pot. For instance, it is famous as the birthplace of jazz music. The biggest festival is Mardi Gras, which is a time of great festivity with many flamboyant parades. The local gastronomy is also famous, especially for dishes like gumbo and sweet, fried beignet pastries. A unique custom is the "second line" parade, which is a joyful street celebration.

The city's identity is a direct result of its challenging geography. Because it is built on a low-lying delta, the city has always faced the threat of flooding, which has fostered a culture of both celebration and resilience. In contrast to many American cities, its French and Spanish colonial history has given it a unique architectural style, evident in the famous wrought-iron balconies, hidden courtyards, and above-ground cemetery designs. This historical foundation created a culture that is more collective and expressive, a spirit that continues today despite ongoing struggles with coastal erosion and social inequality.

La Paz, Bolivia

Vocabulary

  1. altitude /ˈæltɪˌtjuːd/: The city's high altitude can be challenging for visitors.
  2. canyon /ˈkænjən/: La Paz is built within a deep canyon created by the Choqueyapu River.
  3. Altiplano /ˌæltɪˈplɑːnəʊ/: The city spreads out onto the high Altiplano plateau.
  4. indigenous /ɪnˈdɪdʒənəs/: La Paz has a large indigenous population, primarily Aymara and Quechua.
  5. Aymara /ˌaɪməˈrɑː/: The Aymara culture is a very visible and important part of the city's identity.
  6. cholita /tʃəˈliːtə/: A cholita is an Aymara woman known for her traditional dress of a bowler hat and layered skirts.
  7. Mi Teleférico /miː ˌtɛləˈfɛrɪkəʊ/: The Mi Teleférico is the city's modern cable car public transport system.
  8. Witches' Market /ˈwɪtʃɪz ˈmɑːrkɪt/: At the Witches' Market, vendors sell traditional herbs and folk remedies.
  9. salteña /sælˈteɪnjə/: A salteña is a popular baked pastry filled with a savory stew.
  10. administrative capital /ədˈmɪnɪstrətɪv ˈkæpɪtəl/: La Paz is the world's highest administrative capital.
  11. colonial /kəˈləʊniəl/: The city center has many well-preserved colonial-era buildings.
  12. vibrant /ˈvaɪbrənt/: It is a vibrant city with bustling markets and colorful street life.
  13. topography /təˈpɒɡrəfi/: The city's unique topography creates stunning views but also transportation challenges.
  14. bustling /ˈbʌslɪŋ/: The streets are always bustling with activity from morning until night.
  15. resilience /rɪˈzɪliəns/: The people show great resilience in adapting to the high-altitude environment.
  16. fusion /ˈfjuːʒən/: The city's culture is a fusion of indigenous and European traditions.
  17. elevation /ˌelɪˈveɪʃən/: The extreme elevation difference within the city is over 1,000 meters.
  18. metropolis /məˈtrɒpəlɪs/: It is a sprawling metropolis that fills the canyon and climbs its walls.
  19. cable car /ˈkeɪbəl kɑːr/: The cable car system is the best way to see the city's layout.
  20. plateau /plæˈtəʊ/: The neighboring city of El Alto is located on the flat plateau above La Paz.

Warm-up Questions

  1. La Paz is famous for its extremely high altitude. How do you think living at such a high elevation, similar to Tibet, would affect a person's daily health, diet, and activities?
  2. The city uses a modern cable car system for public transport because of its steep mountains. What innovative transportation solutions have you seen in Chinese cities to solve problems of geography or crowding?
  3. The article mentions the "Witches' Market" where traditional remedies are sold. How does this compare to the role of Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) in modern Chinese society?
  4. The "cholita" women are a strong symbol of indigenous identity. Can you think of a style of clothing or a cultural symbol that represents a specific group or region in China?
  5. La Paz is a fusion of indigenous and Spanish colonial cultures. Which period in Chinese history do you think created the most interesting fusion of cultures (e.g., the Tang Dynasty with the Silk Road)? Why?

Reading Passage

La Paz is the administrative capital of Bolivia, a country in South America. It is famous because it is the world's highest capital city. The city has a very high altitude, so the air is thin. Many indigenous people live in La Paz. The city is built in a big valley, and it has a modern cable car system for transport called Mi Teleférico. This system helps people travel up and down the steep hills easily.

Furthermore, the city's unique topography, which is set in a deep canyon that spreads onto the high Altiplano plateau, makes it visually stunning. La Paz has a rich history that blends its pre-colonial past with Spanish influence. For example, you can see this cultural fusion in its bustling street markets. The most famous of these is the Witches' Market, where vendors sell traditional herbs and spiritual items. The city is also known for its vibrant street life, where you can see Aymara women, known as cholitas, in their traditional clothing. Moreover, a popular local food is the salteña, a savory baked pastry which is enjoyed by many.

This sprawling metropolis shows remarkable resilience, thriving despite its extreme conditions. The city's challenging elevation is the direct cause of its most innovative effect: the creation of the Mi Teleférico to solve transportation problems. In contrast to many flat, sprawling capitals, La Paz's growth is vertical, climbing the canyon walls. This geography creates a daily struggle for its population but also results in breathtaking views. The city powerfully balances its identity, which is evident in the survival of ancient traditions alongside the development of a state-of-the-art transit system, showcasing a city defined by both its powerful heritage and its forward-thinking spirit.

Svalbard, Norway

Vocabulary

  1. archipelago /ˌɑːrkɪˈpeləɡəʊ/: Svalbard is a Norwegian archipelago located between mainland Norway and the North Pole.
  2. glacier /ˈɡleɪʃər/: More than half of the land is covered by vast glaciers.
  3. polar bear /ˈpəʊlər ber/: It is mandatory to carry a rifle outside settlements to protect against polar bears.
  4. midnight sun /ˈmɪdnaɪt sʌn/: During the summer, the midnight sun shines 24 hours a day.
  5. polar night /ˈpəʊlər naɪt/: The polar night, a period of continuous darkness, lasts for months.
  6. seed vault /ˈsiːd vɔːlt/: The Global Seed Vault stores backup copies of the world's crop seeds.
  7. sovereignty /ˈsɒvrənti/: Norway has sovereignty over Svalbard, but other nations have rights there.
  8. treaty /ˈtriːti/: The unique Svalbard Treaty of 1920 governs the archipelago.
  9. mining /ˈmaɪnɪŋ/: Coal mining was the historic foundation of the community.
  10. Longyearbyen /lɒŋjɪəˈbjɜːn/: Longyearbyen is the world's northernmost significant settlement.
  11. demilitarized /diːˈmɪlɪtəraɪzd/: The archipelago is a demilitarized zone.
  12. tundra /ˈtʌndrə/: The landscape consists of stark and beautiful arctic tundra.
  13. permafrost /ˈpɜːrməfrɒst/: The Seed Vault is built deep inside a mountain in the permafrost.
  14. expedition /ˌekspəˈdɪʃən/: Svalbard is a popular starting point for North Pole expeditions.
  15. visa-free /ˈviːzə friː/: Uniquely, the entire archipelago is a visa-free zone for all treaty signatories.
  16. high-Arctic /haɪ ˈɑːrktɪk/: The archipelago has a high-Arctic climate moderated by the North Atlantic Current.
  17. research /rɪˈsɜːrtʃ/: It has become a major hub for international arctic research.
  18. Solfestuka /sɔːlˈfɛstʊkə/: Solfestuka is a week-long festival celebrating the return of the sun after the polar night.
  19. spitsbergen /spɪtsˈbɜːrɡən/: The largest island of the archipelago is named Spitsbergen.
  20. wilderness /ˈwɪldərnəs/: It is one of Europe's last great wilderness areas.

Warm-up Questions

  1. Svalbard is an international, visa-free zone governed by a treaty. Can you think of any special economic zones or international ports in China (like Shanghai, Shenzhen, or Hong Kong)? What are the benefits of having such zones?
  2. The Global Seed Vault in Svalbard protects the world’s agricultural diversity. Why is it important for humanity to preserve things like seeds or cultural artifacts for the distant future?
  3. In Svalbard, the sun doesn't set in summer and doesn't rise in winter. How are light and darkness important in traditional Chinese philosophy, such as the concept of Yin and Yang (阴阳)?
  4. People must carry rifles for protection from polar bears. Describe a situation where people in China must take special precautions because of local wildlife or natural conditions.
  5. Svalbard shifted its economy from coal mining to scientific research and tourism. How can a city or region successfully change its main industry? Can you think of an example in China?

Reading Passage

Svalbard is a Norwegian archipelago in the Arctic Ocean. It is located between Norway and the North Pole. The land is covered in mountains, glacier ice, and treeless tundra. The climate is high-Arctic but not as cold as other places so far north. The main town is called Longyearbyen. In the past, people came here for coal mining and hunting.

Today, Svalbard is a global hub for scientific research and tourism, and it is a popular starting point for any polar expedition. Life here is shaped by its extreme environment, such as the summer midnight sun and the dark polar night. A special festival called Solfestuka celebrates the sun's return. A unique rule is that people must carry rifles outside of towns for protection against the large polar bear population. The largest island in the group is named Spitsbergen.

This location is presented as a place of both extreme wilderness and international cooperation. A key cause of its unique social structure is the Svalbard Treaty, which grants Norwegian sovereignty but allows the archipelago to be demilitarized and visa-free for citizens of many nations. In contrast to a typical national territory, Svalbard functions as a global commons, though this open status creates challenges for managing its fragile environment. The Global Seed Vault, a facility built into the permafrost as a safeguard for all humanity, illustrates the forward-thinking and cooperative spirit that defines this remarkable place.

Hobart, Australia

Vocabulary

  1. Tasmania /tæzˈmeɪniə/: Hobart is the capital city of the island state of Tasmania.
  2. harbor /ˈhɑːrbər/: The city is set around one of the deepest natural harbors in the world.
  3. penal colony /ˈpiːnəl ˈkɒləni/: It was founded in 1804 as a British penal colony.
  4. sandstone /ˈsændstəʊn/: Historic sandstone warehouses line the city's waterfront.
  5. Mount Wellington /maʊnt ˈwelɪŋtən/: The city is dominated by the dramatic backdrop of Mount Wellington.
  6. provocative /prəˈvɒkətɪv/: The MONA museum is famous for its provocative and unconventional art.
  7. culinary /ˈkʌlɪnəri/: Hobart has a thriving culinary scene focused on local produce.
  8. estuary /ˈestʃuəri/: The city is situated on the estuary of the Derwent River.
  9. compact /kəmˈpækt/: Hobart is a compact city, making it easy to explore on foot.
  10. maritime /ˈmærɪtaɪm/: The city has a rich maritime history.
  11. yacht race /jɒt reɪs/: It is the finishing point for the famous Sydney to Hobart yacht race.
  12. temperate /ˈtempərət/: The city enjoys a mild, temperate maritime climate.
  13. heritage /ˈherɪtɪdʒ/: The convict heritage of the region is a UNESCO World Heritage site.
  14. vibrancy /ˈvaɪbrənsi/: The opening of MONA brought a new cultural vibrancy to the city.
  15. scallop pie /ˈskɒləp paɪ/: The scallop pie is a famous and unique Tasmanian culinary delight.
  16. pristine /prɪˈstiːn/: Hobart is a gateway to Tasmania's pristine wilderness areas.
  17. colonial /kəˈləʊniəl/: Battery Point is a charming neighborhood with well-preserved colonial architecture.
  18. Dark Mofo /dɑːrk ˈmoʊfoʊ/: Dark Mofo is a popular and controversial winter festival celebrating darkness.
  19. gourmet /ɡʊərˈmeɪ/: The Salamanca Market is a paradise for gourmet food lovers.
  20. renaissance /rəˈneɪsəns/: Hobart has experienced a cultural and economic renaissance in recent years.

Warm-up Questions

  1. Hobart is the capital of an island state, Tasmania. How is the culture and identity of people on an island (like Hainan or Taiwan) different from that on the mainland?
  2. The MONA museum is described as "provocative" and helped give the city a new identity. Do you think modern, and sometimes controversial, art is important for a city's development? Why or why not?
  3. Hobart's history as a penal colony is now a UNESCO heritage site. How should a country remember and present the difficult or dark parts of its history?
  4. The city is a gateway to "pristine wilderness." How important is it for China to protect its own wilderness areas, like those in Tibet or Xinjiang, from development?
  5. The article mentions a special "scallop pie." Describe a unique snack or street food from your hometown that a visitor might find surprising or delicious.

Reading Passage

Hobart is the capital city of Tasmania, an island state of Australia. It is a compact city on the estuary of the Derwent River. The city has a deep natural harbor and is located next to the impressive Mount Wellington. The weather is mild because of its temperate maritime climate. Hobart has a long history and was founded in 1804 as a British penal colony for prisoners.

The city's culture today is a mix of its history and modern art, which has given it a new vibrancy. For example, the famous Museum of Old and New Art (MONA) is known for its provocative art. The city also hosts a major winter festival called Dark Mofo. Hobart is famous for its culinary scene, especially its fresh seafood and unique local dishes like the scallop pie. The historic waterfront, with its old sandstone buildings and charming colonial architecture, is a popular place for locals and tourists.

The city has transformed itself from a remote historical outpost into a dynamic cultural destination, experiencing a true cultural renaissance. The establishment of the MONA museum was a direct cause of this change, and the effect was a boom in tourism and a new identity for the city's small population. In contrast to mainland Australian cities like Sydney, Hobart offers a more intimate connection to both history and nature. This is evident in its role as a gateway to pristine wilderness, its hosting of the world-class Sydney to Hobart yacht race, and its reputation as a center for gourmet food. The city's main struggle now is balancing this new popularity with the preservation of its UNESCO-listed convict heritage and quiet charm.

Carcassonne, France

Vocabulary

  1. fortified /ˈfɔːrtɪfaɪd/: Carcassonne is a famous example of a medieval fortified city.
  2. citadel /ˈsɪtədəl/: The historic citadel, known as the Cité, is a UNESCO World Heritage site.
  3. medieval /ˌmediˈiːvəl/: Walking through the Cité feels like stepping back into medieval times.
  4. ramparts /ˈræmpɑːrts/: The city is enclosed by massive double walls and 52 towers.
  5. drawbridge /ˈdrɔːbrɪdʒ/: You can still see the ancient drawbridge at the main gate.
  6. restoration /ˌrestəˈreɪʃən/: The city underwent extensive restoration work in the 19th century.
  7. UNESCO /juːˈneskəʊ/: Its historical importance is recognized by its UNESCO status.
  8. fortress /ˈfɔːrtrəs/: The fortress has a commanding view of the surrounding plains.
  9. strategic /strəˈtiːdʒɪk/: Its location was highly strategic, controlling trade routes between the Atlantic and Mediterranean.
  10. turret /ˈtʌrət/: Each turret on the wall offered a defensive position for archers.
  11. cobblestone /ˈkɒblstəʊn/: The narrow cobblestone streets are filled with shops and restaurants.
  12. cassoulet /ˌkæsʊˈleɪ/: Cassoulet, a rich, slow-cooked bean stew with meat, is the region's signature dish.
  13. Occitanie /ˌɒksɪˈtɑːni/: Carcassonne is located in the Occitanie region of southern France.
  14. imposing /ɪmˈpəʊzɪŋ/: The imposing walls of the Cité can be seen from miles away.
  15. Canal du Midi /kəˈnæl duː miːˈdiː/: The Canal du Midi, another UNESCO site, runs through the lower city.
  16. heritage /ˈherɪtɪdʒ/: The city is a vital part of France's architectural heritage.
  17. bastion /ˈbæstiən/: The bastion at the corner of the wall was heavily armed.
  18. fairytale /ˈfeəriteɪl/: With its towers and ramparts, the city looks like something from a fairytale.
  19. Cathar /ˈkæθɑːr/: The region was a stronghold of the Cathar religious movement in the Middle Ages.
  20. vineyard /ˈvɪnjərd/: The region around Carcassonne is famous for its extensive vineyards.

Warm-up Questions

  1. Carcassonne is a perfectly preserved medieval fortified city. How does it compare to ancient walled cities in China, like Xi'an or Pingyao? What is the value of such preservation?
  2. The fortress looks like something from a "fairytale." Many Chinese stories feature brave heroes, martial arts, and epic battles (wuxia/武侠). What elements make a castle or fortress seem romantic or heroic to you?
  3. The city's location was very strategic for controlling trade. Think of the ancient Silk Road. How did strategic locations like Dunhuang or Kashgar become important centers for trade and culture?
  4. The article mentions the region's vineyards. Many places in China are famous for a specific agricultural product, like tea from Hangzhou or rice from the Northeast. How does a famous local product shape a region's identity?
  5. If you could live in a perfectly restored ancient city, what would be the best part and what would be the biggest challenge of daily life?

Reading Passage

Carcassonne is a fortified city in the Occitanie region of southern France. It is a famous medieval city. It sits on a hill, which was a very strategic location in the past. The city has a long history and was once a stronghold for the Cathar religious group. Today, the area is also known for its many vineyards that produce wine.

The city has two parts, but it is most famous for its upper city, which is a giant fortress called the Cité de Carcassonne. This amazing citadel, which is a UNESCO World Heritage site, has imposing double walls, a defensive bastion, and 52 towers, each with a pointed turret. Inside the walls, you can walk on old cobblestone streets. Another UNESCO site, the Canal du Midi, also runs through the city.

This city transports visitors to a real-life fairytale setting. The primary cause for its modern appearance was the extensive restoration work in the 19th century; the effect is a perfectly preserved example of medieval military architecture. In contrast to many historic sites which are ruins, Carcassonne is a living town that struggles with the pressure of its popularity. The city's architectural heritage is undeniable as you cross the drawbridge and see the massive ramparts. This history is complemented by a rich local culture, famous for its food, like the hearty stew called cassoulet.

Utrecht, Netherlands

Vocabulary

  1. canal /kəˈnæl/: Utrecht is famous for its unique inner-city canal structure.
  2. wharf /wɔːrf/: The city has a system of wharves and cellars at water level.
  3. Dom Tower /dɒm ˈtaʊər/: The Dom Tower is the tallest church tower in the Netherlands.
  4. bustling /ˈbʌslɪŋ/: Utrecht is a bustling university city with a youthful energy.
  5. cellar /ˈselər/: Many of the old wharf cellars have been converted into cafes and restaurants.
  6. cyclist /ˈsaɪklɪst/: The city is a paradise for cyclists, with extensive bike paths.
  7. terrace /ˈterəs/: In summer, the canal-side terraces are filled with people.
  8. central /ˌsentrəl/: Its central location makes it a vital transport hub for the country.
  9. gothic /ˈɡɒθɪk/: The Dom Tower is a classic example of Gothic architecture.
  10. hub /hʌb/: Utrecht is a major railway hub in the Netherlands.
  11. historic /hɪˈstɒrɪk/: The historic city center is largely pedestrian-friendly.
  12. conurbation /ˌkɒnɜːˈbeɪʃən/: It is part of the large Randstad conurbation in the Netherlands.
  13. laid-back /ˌleɪdˈbæk/: The city has a more laid-back atmosphere than the capital.
  14. split-level /ˌsplɪtˈlevəl/: The split-level design of the canals is unique in the world.
  15. vibrant /ˈvaɪbrənt/: The large student population gives the city a vibrant, creative feel.
  16. intimate /ˈɪntɪmət/: The canal structure creates a more intimate setting than in other Dutch cities.
  17. Rietveld Schröder House /riːtˈvɛld ˈʃroʊdər haʊs/: The Rietveld Schröder House is a famous example of modern architecture and a UNESCO site.
  18. hofjes /ˈhɒfjəs/: Hidden courtyards, known as 'hofjes', can be found throughout the city.
  19. innovative /ɪnˈnɒvətɪv/: The city is known for its innovative architecture and urban planning.
  20. broodje /broʊdˈjə/: A "broodje Mario" is a famous, hearty Italian sandwich that is an Utrecht specialty.

Warm-up Questions

  1. Utrecht is famous for its canals, much like Chinese cities such as Suzhou. In your opinion, how do canals and waterways affect the atmosphere and lifestyle of a city?
  2. The city is a paradise for cyclists. China was once known as the "Kingdom of Bicycles." What are the benefits and challenges of having bicycles as a primary mode of transport in a modern city?
  3. Utrecht has a large student population that gives it a vibrant, creative feel. How do universities and young people contribute to the culture and energy of a city like Beijing or Nanjing?
  4. The Rietveld Schröder House is a UNESCO site for its modern, innovative design. Usually we think of old sites as heritage. Do you believe modern buildings can be as culturally important as ancient ones? Why?
  5. Utrecht is described as more "laid-back" than the capital, Amsterdam. How would you compare the atmosphere of a major capital like Beijing with another large but perhaps more laid-back city in China (e.g., Chengdu)?

Reading Passage

Utrecht is a large city in the Netherlands, Europe. It is in a very central location, which makes it an important transportation hub for the country. The city has a long and historic past. Its most famous landmark is the tall, Gothic-style Dom Tower. Utrecht is also a bustling university city with a vibrant and youthful feeling. You can also find many peaceful, hidden courtyards called hofjes.

The city is well-known for its unique canal system, which is built on a split-level design. Along the water, there is a lower wharf where old storage cellars have been turned into restaurants and cafes with a lovely outdoor terrace. This design creates a very cozy and intimate atmosphere. Utrecht is also a paradise for any cyclist because of its excellent bike paths. While it is a big city, part of a larger conurbation, it has a more laid-back feel than the capital, Amsterdam.

Utrecht is presented as a charming and innovative alternative to more famous Dutch cities. Its medieval trading history is the cause of its unique canal life, which in turn creates the effect of a lively social scene on the waterside terraces. In contrast to typical canals where houses meet the water directly, Utrecht's design allows for a unique public space. The city's culture balances this history with modernity, a fact exemplified by the UNESCO-listed Rietveld Schröder House. A key struggle for this popular city is managing population growth and housing while maintaining the historic character and high quality of life that make it so attractive, a challenge seen even in its local food culture with the immense popularity of a simple broodje sandwich.
