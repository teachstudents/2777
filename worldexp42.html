Part 1: A Trailblazing Physicist (Reading)

Chien-Shiung Wu (1912-1997) was a pioneering experimental physicist who made profound contributions to our understanding of the universe. Born in China, she emigrated to the United States in 1936 to pursue graduate studies at the University of California, Berkeley. During World War II, her expertise was crucial to the Manhattan Project, where she developed a process to separate uranium isotopes, a vital step in creating the atomic bomb.

However, her most significant and famous work came after the war. In 1956, she conducted the landmark "Wu Experiment." At the time, a fundamental law of physics was the Law of Conservation of Parity. Wu's brilliant experiment, which involved observing the beta decay of radioactive Cobalt-60 at near absolute zero, proved this long-held law was wrong in the context of the weak nuclear force.

This discovery was revolutionary, completely upending theoretical physics. The theorists who had proposed the idea of parity violation, Lee and Yang, were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1957. In a significant and widely criticized injustice, Wu, who designed and executed the incredibly difficult experiment that proved their theory, was excluded by the Nobel committee. Despite this, she received many other prestigious awards, including the Wolf Prize in Physics in 1978. Her story is not just one of scientific genius, but also a powerful lesson about the nature of scientific credit, perseverance, and the historical biases that have often overlooked the contributions of women in science. She is often called the "First Lady of Physics" or the "Chinese Marie Curie" for her immense resilience and contributions to experimental physics at institutions like Columbia University.
